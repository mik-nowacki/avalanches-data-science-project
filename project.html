Description

(source)
Attention: this data is not updated after 2022 anymore. This data collection contains information concerning all accidents by snow avalanches causing at least one fatality in Switzerland. The data set commences on 01/10/1995. After the completion of a hydrological year, the new data is added. The following information is provided:

  • avalanche.id = avalanche identifier
  • date = date of the accident (YYYY-MM-DD)
  • date.quality = accuracy of the date in range of days before and after
  • hydrological.year = 12-month period from 1 October to 30 September
  • canton = Cantons of Switzerland (Member states of the Swiss Confederation)
  • local.name = name of the locality
  • start.zone.coordinates.x/y = start zone of the avalanche (Swiss coordinate system, approximately in the middle of start zone)
  • coordinates.quality = accuracy of the coordinates in meters
  • start.zone.elevation = elevation (in meters above sea level, approximately in the middle of start zone)
  • start.zone.slope.aspect = slope aspect (main orientation of start zone)
  • start.zone.inclination = slope inclination (in degree, steepest point within start zone)
  • forecasted.dangerlevel = forecasted avalanche danger level (1-5)
  • number.dead = number of dead persons
  • number.caught = number of caught persons
  • number.fully.buried = number of fully buried persons
  • activity = activity/location of the accident party at the time of the incident

Step 3 & 4. Formulate hypotheses:¶

Hypothesis 1.¶

At the 95% confidence level, test if there is a significant difference in the number of accidents between periods 1995-2010 and 2011 - 2021 during peak season (February)

Null Hypothesis ($H_0$): There is no significant difference in the mean number of accidents between the two periods (1995–2010 and 2011–2021) during peak season.

  • $H_0$: $μ_1 = μ_2$

Alternative Hypothesis ($H_A$): There is a significant difference in the mean number of accidents betwen the two periods.

  • $H_A$: $μ_1$≠$μ_2$

These two samples are independent and with different variances. To perform the hypothesis test the two-sample t-test will be used.

print('Period 1 variance: ', period1['number.combined'].var(ddof=1))
print('Period 2 variance: ', period2['number.combined'].var(ddof=1))

# mean2 = period2['number.combined'].mean()

The p-value of 0.083 is greater than alpha = 0.05 which means we fail to reject the null hypothesis. There is no strong evidence to suggest a significant difference in the number of avalanche accidents during February between the two periods (1995–2010 and 2011–2021).

This test shows how dangerous mountains are. Despite all the cutting-edge technology and growing awareness among people who go out in the mountains accidents still happen and do not seem to be on decline. Weather is very upredictable and with each year it gets even worse. Climate change has a strong impact on its fluctuation which makes it difficult to accurately forecast for snowfalls and snowstorms.

Hypothesis 2.¶

At the 95% confidence level, test if there is a difference in the number of accidents between expeditions that start lower at than 2500m and higer than 2500m.

Null Hypothesis $H_0$: There is no difference in the mean number of accidents between expeditions starting below 2500m and above 2500m.

  • $H_0$: $μ_{below}=μ_{above}$

Alternative Hypothesis $H_A$: There is a difference in the mean number of accidents.

  • $H_A$: $μ_{below}$ ≠ $μ_{above}$

Step 6: Final conclusion and potential pitfalls:¶

Final conclusion¶

The analysis of avalanche accidents in Swiss Alps has revealed valuable insights into the patterns and factors contributing to these tragic events.

Visualisation of data showed the darker side of skiing and helped identify key aspects of accidents happening. Plot 2 succesfully portrayed how the winter season changes. Plot 4 and Plot 5 helped to determine which aspects of the mountain are the most dangerous.

Hypothesis tests indicated that humanity still has not developed enough countermeasures that would reduce the number of accidents in the mountains.

Potential pitfalls¶

  • Data limitations: missing values in columns such as slope inclination or danger levels have reduced the analysis potential of some aspects of the data
  • Hypothesis testing constraints: Assumptions of normality may not fully hold, impacting the results
  • Other variables: Snowpack depth, snowfall reports during that month / year and experience level of the expedition party (though hard to realistically measure) could expand the scope of analysis and improve credibility of the results.
